Manufactured Science: The Monsanto Papers

Dec 11, 2025 â€¢ 12 min read

Glyphosate is the most widely used herbicide in human history. Marketed as "Roundup," it changed modern agriculture. But beneath the success story lies a dark narrative of corporate influence, suppressed data, and questionable science.

In a gripping investigation, Veritasium uncovers how Monsanto (now Bayer) allegedly ghostwrote scientific papers, bullied dissenters, and planted stories to protect their billion-dollar product from cancer classifications.

See also: IARC Monograph on Glyphosate.

1. The IARC Classification

In 2015, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified glyphosate as "probably carcinogenic to humans." This sparked a massive counter-offensive from Monsanto. Internal emails later revealed a strategy to "orchestrate an outcry" against the IARC findings.

2. Ghostwriting & "Independent" Science

The "Monsanto Papers," released during litigation, showed company scientists editing and even writing parts of papers that were published under the names of independent academics. This created an echo chamber of safety assurances that appeared to come from neutral third parties but were, in fact, crafted by the manufacturer.

  • The Strategy: Identify "friendly" scientists to lend their names to industry-written drafts.
  • The Goal: Create a body of "scientific literature" that regulators would rely on to keep the product on the market.

3. The Human Cost

While the legal battles rage on with billion-dollar settlements, the question of public health remains. Whether glyphosate is definitely carcinogenic is still debated, but the tactics used to influence that debate are now clear. It serves as a stark warning about the intersection of profit and peer review.

Conclusion

Science relies on transparency. When that transparency is manipulated by corporate interests, the very foundation of public trust erodes. The story of Monsanto is not just about a chemical; it's about the integrity of the scientific process itself.
